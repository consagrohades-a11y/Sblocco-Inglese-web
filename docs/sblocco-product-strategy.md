# Sblocco Inglese — Product Strategy & Learning Architecture

_Last updated: 2026-06-28_

This document is the current source of truth for the direction of Sblocco Inglese. It combines the product strategy, website architecture, exercise architecture, diagnostic logic, tag philosophy, and build priorities.

It should be updated whenever the strategic or technical direction changes.

---

## 1. North Star

Sblocco Inglese is not just an English course website.

It is a practical English-learning platform for Italian adults who need to use English in real situations.

The long-term goal is to build a web app that can work almost independently from live courses while still integrating those courses in a commercially powerful way.

Core promise:

> Sblocco Inglese diagnoses what blocks your English, gives you a practical path, and trains you to use English in real situations.

The platform should:

1. diagnose the learner's real blockers;
2. give a clear learning path;
3. train through exercises, trainers, repetition, and real-life scenarios;
4. recommend the right paid path when human guidance is useful;
5. support courses, audits, and premium 1:1 work;
6. become a scalable product rather than a collection of pages.

The site should make the learner think:

> This finally shows me what is wrong with my English and what I need to do next.

## 2. Strategic Positioning

Do not position Sblocco Inglese as generic “English courses.”

Position it as:

> A practical English platform for Italian adults who need English in real situations.

Main audiences:

- people who understand English but freeze when speaking;
- workers who need English professionally;
- people preparing interviews;
- hospitality, tourism, restaurant, hotel, event, and customer-service workers;
- students or adults who want to understand their real level and gaps;
- advanced speakers who need precision, authority, nuance, and correction.

The website should ask:

> Where do you get blocked?

Not only:

> What level are you?

The platform should route users through both:

```text
level path + applied path
```

Example:

```text
Estimated level: A2/B1
Core path: A2 Everyday Communication or B1 Fluency
Applied path: Hospitality Starter
Main blocker: question formation under pressure
```

## 3. The Three-Way Strategy

The business should be built around three connected layers.

### 3.1 Web App / Platform

The platform is the scalable asset.

It should include:

- grammar diagnostics;
- level-based grammar and communication paths;
- vocabulary and expression trainers;
- SRS repetition;
- business English trainers;
- hospitality English trainers;
- interview preparation;
- speaking prompts;
- progress tracking;
- diagnostic feedback;
- recommendation logic;
- course and audit CTAs.

The platform should make the student feel:

> I know exactly what is wrong and what I need to do next.

### 3.2 Courses / Guided Paths

Courses provide structure, explanation, deadlines, correction, and accountability.

They should be practical paths, not generic English courses:

- Fluency Foundations;
- Business English;
- Interview English;
- Hospitality English;
- Advanced English Lab.

Courses should be integrated with the platform, not separate from it.

### 3.3 Premium Human Support

Human support is the high-value layer.

Possible offers:

- €39 English audit;
- interview sprint;
- business English package;
- hospitality English package;
- fluency reset;
- premium 1:1 path;
- advanced C1/C2 correction lab.

The app handles structure and repetition. Human support handles correction, confidence, nuance, and transformation.

## 5. Level Philosophy

Levels should be defined by output ability, not only by grammar topics.

The question is:

> What can the learner actually do by the end of this level?

### 5.1 A1 — First Speaking Control

A1 must enable first speaking, not just grammar recognition.

Goal:

> By the end of A1, the student can create simple spoken sentences, ask and answer basic questions, and survive simple interactions.

A1 should include the grammar needed to start speaking:

- be: am / is / are;
- subject pronouns;
- possessives;
- present simple basics;
- questions with be;
- questions with do / does;
- negatives: don’t / doesn’t, isn’t / aren’t;
- have / have got;
- there is / there are;
- can / can’t;
- would like as a useful chunk;
- imperatives;
- articles and plurals;
- this / that / these / those;
- basic prepositions;
- basic adjectives;
- and / but / because.

A1 speaking outcomes:

- introduce yourself;
- say where you are from;
- say what you do;
- talk about routine;
- say what you like, want, and need;
- ask basic questions;
- answer basic questions;
- use numbers, time, and prices;
- make basic requests;
- explain simple problems.

Outcome:

> I can speak in short but usable sentences.

### 5.2 A2 — Everyday Communication

Goal:

> The student can talk about daily life, past events, future plans, needs, preferences, and simple problems.

Focus:

- past simple;
- future plans;
- requests;
- travel;
- appointments;
- restaurants;
- shopping;
- simple opinions;
- comparing options;
- simple problem-solving.

Outcome:

> I can participate in common everyday conversations.

### 5.3 B1 — Independent Conversation

Goal:

> The student can maintain a conversation without collapsing.

Focus:

- storytelling;
- opinions;
- experiences;
- explanations;
- present perfect;
- past continuous;
- conditionals;
- used to;
- linking ideas;
- longer answers;
- speaking without translating every sentence.

Outcome:

> I can communicate independently, but I still need more accuracy and confidence.

### 5.4 B2 — Professional Independence

Goal:

> The student can use English in work situations without sounding basic or confused.

Focus:

- meetings;
- calls;
- emails;
- interviews;
- explaining work;
- giving opinions;
- handling problems;
- presenting ideas;
- professional vocabulary;
- accuracy under pressure.

Outcome:

> I can function professionally in English.

B2 is commercially important.

### 5.5 C1 — Precision and Authority

Goal:

> The student can express complex ideas clearly, naturally, and professionally.

Focus:

- nuance;
- persuasion;
- advanced grammar;
- softening;
- argumentation;
- professional writing;
- leadership language;
- advanced conversation;
- domain-specific English.

Outcome:

> I sound competent, articulate, and credible.

### 5.6 C2 — Style, Nuance, and High-Level Performance

Goal:

> The student can adapt English to high-stakes contexts with precision and sophistication.

Focus:

- rhetoric;
- register control;
- idiomatic precision;
- cultural nuance;
- executive communication;
- debate;
- advanced writing;
- advanced listening nuance;
- style correction.

Outcome:

> I do not just speak correctly. I control tone, implication, and effect.

C2 should likely be a premium/human-supported layer rather than a cheap self-study product.

## 6. Level Pages Must Be Modular

A level page must not be a fixed school-style grammar dump.

Each level page should contain:

```text
Core exercises
Path-specific applications
Diagnostics
Recommendations
```

Example A1 page:

```text
A1 — First Speaking Control

Core modules:
1. Introduce yourself
2. Ask and answer basic questions
3. Talk about routine
4. Say what you want/need
5. Use numbers, prices, and time
6. Handle simple problems

Applied modules:
- A1 Hospitality: greet guests, prices, directions
- A1 Business: introduce your job, simple emails
- A1 Interview: basic personal answers

Diagnostics:
- A1 placement check
- A1 speaking readiness
- A1 final survival test
```

The level is a container, not a hardcoded course.

Data shape:

```js
{
  level: "a1",
  sections: [
    { type: "core", unitIds: [...] },
    { type: "path", path: "hospitality", unitIds: [...] },
    { type: "path", path: "business", unitIds: [...] },
    { type: "path", path: "interview", unitIds: [...] }
  ]
}
```

If a future track appears, for example Travel English, it should be added as data:

```js
{ type: "path", path: "travel", unitIds: [...] }
```

No page rewrite should be needed.

## 12. Exercise Architecture

Every exercise should follow a shared schema.

Example:

```js
{
  id: "a1-present-simple-questions-01",
  level: "a1",
  track: "core",
  unit: "basic-questions",
  type: "gap-fill",
  purpose: "controlled-production",
  title: "Ask basic questions",
  instructions: "Complete the questions.",
  items: [...]
}
```

Each item should include structured diagnostic metadata.

Example:

```js
{
  id: "item-001",
  type: "blank",
  prompt: "Where ___ you live?",
  correctAnswers: ["do"],
  answer: "do",
  baseForm: "live",

  diagnostic: {
    skills: ["question-formation", "sentence-control"],
    grammar: ["present-simple", "auxiliary-do"],
    errorPatterns: ["missing-auxiliary", "be-do-confusion", "italian-word-order-transfer"],
    contexts: ["general-speaking", "hospitality", "interview"],
    production: "controlled-production",
    severity: 3
  },

  feedback: {
    correct: "Correct. With a normal verb like live, use do in the question.",
    incorrect: "Use do because live is a normal verb. Are is only for be, not for normal verbs."
  },

  explanation: "Present Simple questions use do/does + subject + base verb."
}
```

The same item should be usable for:

- exercise correction;
- diagnostic scoring;
- student progress;
- recommendations;
- weak-area reports;
- future dashboard.

## 14. Diagnostic Philosophy

The diagnostic cannot be a basic quiz that says:

> You need to review auxiliaries.

That is too shallow.

The diagnostic must identify:

```text
wrong answer → error pattern → underlying cause → affected skill → affected contexts → recommendation
```

Example mistake:

```text
Prompt: Where ___ you live?
Student: are
Correct: do
```

Weak feedback:

> Wrong. Use do.

Strong diagnostic feedback:

> You used are because you are treating English questions as if be can form every question. In English, normal verbs need do/does in present simple questions. This pattern affects basic speaking, hospitality guest questions, and interviews.

Possible tags:

```text
skill: question formation
grammar: present simple
error pattern: be/do confusion
production mode: controlled production
likely cause: Italian word order transfer
affected contexts: general speaking, hospitality, interview
severity: high if repeated
```

The goal is accurate, detailed understanding of the error, not generic scoring.

## 15. Diagnostic Types

The diagnostic should not be identical across all levels and exercises.

We need several diagnostic types.

### 15.1 Entry Diagnostic

Purpose:

> Where should this person start?

It estimates:

- level range;
- main blockers;
- use-case;
- path relevance.

### 15.2 Level Diagnostic

Purpose:

> What exactly is weak inside this level?

A1 diagnostic checks:

- basic sentence formation;
- be;
- present simple;
- can;
- basic questions;
- simple answers;
- basic speaking readiness.

B2 diagnostic checks:

- professional communication;
- argument structure;
- register;
- complex sentence control;
- meeting/interview clarity.

### 15.3 Path Diagnostic

Purpose:

> Can this person use English in this context?

Hospitality diagnostic checks:

- guest interaction;
- service politeness;
- complaints;
- booking language;
- directions;
- restaurant/hotel vocabulary.

Business diagnostic checks:

- meetings;
- email tone;
- explaining tasks;
- giving updates;
- professional clarity.

Interview diagnostic checks:

- self-introduction;
- career story;
- STAR answers;
- behavioral answers;
- confidence under pressure.

### 15.4 Exercise-Level Diagnostic

Every exercise should update the learner profile.

Example insight:

> This student repeatedly misses do/does questions in hospitality contexts but performs better in general multiple choice.

This is valuable for recommendations and future dashboards.

## 16. Diagnostic Dimensions

Diagnostics should measure at least these dimensions.

### 16.1 Grammar Control

Specific control, not generic grammar knowledge:

- be vs normal verbs;
- do/does questions;
- negatives;
- modal verbs;
- tense control;
- articles;
- plurals;
- word order;
- prepositions;
- pronouns;
- sentence expansion.

### 16.2 Sentence Construction

Can the student build an English sentence?

Recognition is not enough.

The system should distinguish:

```text
recognition → controlled production → free production → applied use
```

A student may choose correctly in multiple choice but fail in production.

### 16.3 Speaking Readiness

Measure:

- can answer quickly?
- can form a sentence without translating?
- can ask back?
- can recover from a mistake?
- can use chunks naturally?
- can handle a short dialogue?

### 16.4 Active Vocabulary

Not just word recognition.

Measure:

- can the student actively use common verbs?
- can they retrieve work/hospitality/interview phrases?
- can they describe problems?
- can they paraphrase when missing a word?

### 16.5 Use-Case Readiness

Detect where the learner needs English:

- general fluency;
- hospitality;
- business;
- interview;
- travel/relocation;
- advanced professional communication.

The result should recommend both:

```text
core level path + applied path
```

## 17. Open Tags With Scoped Registries

Tags must not be a rigid fixed enum.

Wrong:

```js
type ErrorTag = "auxiliary-confusion" | "article-error" | "word-order";
```

This will break when a more precise diagnosis is needed.

Correct principle:

> Open tags with scoped registries and graceful fallback.

### 17.1 Stable Tag Dimensions

The dimensions are stable:

```text
skillTags
grammarTags
errorPatternTags
contextTags
productionTags
levelTags
pathTags
```

The individual tags inside them are flexible and expandable.

Example:

```js
{
  skillTags: ["question-formation", "spoken-sentence-control"],
  grammarTags: ["present-simple", "auxiliary-do"],
  errorPatternTags: ["missing-auxiliary", "italian-word-order-transfer"],
  contextTags: ["hospitality", "guest-interaction"],
  productionTags: ["controlled-production"]
}
```

The code should not break if later we add:

```text
polite-service-softening
wine-description-vocabulary
hesitation-under-pressure
overliteral-italian-translation
```

### 17.2 What Should Be Fixed

These should be stable:

```text
tag dimensions
exercise schema
diagnostic schema
scoring rules
recommendation engine structure
```

### 17.3 What Should Be Flexible

These should be expandable:

```text
specific grammar tags
specific error patterns
specific hospitality tags
specific business tags
specific C1/C2 nuance tags
specific speaking blockers
specific recommendation mappings
```

The architecture should be strict. The diagnostic content should be extensible.

## 18. Scoped Tag Registries

Avoid one giant closed global list.

Use scoped registries:

```text
global tags
  used everywhere

level tags
  A1-specific, A2-specific, B1-specific, etc.

track tags
  hospitality-specific, business-specific, interview-specific

unit tags
  tags that only matter inside a specific unit

exercise/item tags
  very specific local diagnostic metadata
```

Example:

```js
{
  globalTags: {
    skills: ["question-formation", "sentence-control", "active-vocabulary"],
    production: ["recognition", "controlled-production", "free-production", "applied-use"]
  },

  levelTags: {
    a1: {
      grammar: ["be-present", "present-simple-basic", "can-basic"],
      errors: ["be-do-confusion", "missing-auxiliary", "wrong-basic-word-order"]
    }
  },

  trackTags: {
    hospitality: {
      contexts: ["guest-greeting", "check-in", "complaint-handling"],
      errors: ["too-direct-service-language", "missing-politeness-formula"]
    }
  }
}
```

Important:

This is a reference map, not a closed prison.

If an item uses a new tag, the app should not crash.

## 19. How Code Should Treat Tags

The engine should treat tags as strings and aggregate them.

Do not hardcode behavior like:

```js
if (tag === "auxiliary-confusion") {
  showAuxiliaryFeedback();
}
```

That becomes fragile.

Use registry enrichment:

```js
const tagInfo = tagRegistry[tag];

if (tagInfo) {
  use tagInfo.label, explanation, recommendations;
} else {
  keep the tag as raw evidence and use fallback feedback;
}
```

Known tags get richer diagnosis.

Unknown tags still work.

This avoids brittleness.

## 20. Evidence-Based Diagnostics

The system should not assume one mistake equals one diagnosis.

It should collect evidence.

Example evidence object:

```js
{
  tag: "missing-auxiliary",
  evidenceCount: 6,
  contexts: ["general-speaking", "hospitality"],
  productionModes: ["gap-fill", "translation-it-en", "dialogue-gap-fill"],
  severity: "high"
}
```

Then the system can say:

> You repeatedly miss auxiliaries when building questions, especially in production exercises. This is not just recognition weakness; it affects real speaking.

The diagnostic result should become more specific as evidence increases.

## 22. Recommendation Architecture

Recommendations must be specific.

Not:

> Review auxiliaries.

But:

> You need A1/A2 question formation because you repeatedly miss auxiliary do/does when building questions with normal verbs. Start with Ask Basic Questions, then do Hospitality Guest Questions.

A recommendation should include:

1. what is wrong;
2. why it matters;
3. what to practice;
4. which path it affects;
5. what to do next.

Recommendation mapping example:

```js
{
  tag: "be-do-confusion",
  recommendations: [
    "/levels/a1/basic-questions",
    "/levels/a2/question-formation",
    "/tracks/hospitality/guest-questions",
    "/audit"
  ]
}
```

Hospitality example:

```js
{
  tag: "hospitality-complaints",
  recommendations: [
    "/tracks/hospitality/complaints",
    "/courses/hospitality-english",
    "/audit"
  ]
}
```

The system should always answer:

> What should this student do next?

## 23. Diagnostic Result Quality Standard

A diagnostic result should not be a score-only result.

It should include:

```text
estimated level
confidence level of estimate
main blockers
evidence for each blocker
affected contexts
likely causes
recommended level path
recommended applied path
recommended exercises
course/audit CTA
```

Example:

```text
Estimated level: A2/B1

Main blocker:
Question formation under pressure

Evidence:
You missed do/does in 5 out of 7 production questions, especially in dialogue and translation tasks.

Likely cause:
You are applying Italian-style question structure to English and overusing be.

Affected contexts:
General speaking, hospitality guest questions, interviews.

Recommended next step:
Start A1/A2 Question Formation, then Hospitality Guest Questions.
```

### How Long Can We Go Without Backend?

Rough threshold:

```text
0–100 serious users: no database is fine
100–300 users: localStorage still possible, but limitations appear
300+ users or paid membership: backend becomes necessary
```

Backend becomes necessary when users need:

1. accounts;
2. paid locked content;
3. saved progress across devices;
4. teacher dashboard;
5. student history;
6. email result recovery;
7. subscription access;
8. analytics tied to users.

Do not add login/database yet. Build the system so it can later accept a backend.

## 28. Self-Selling Mechanism

The website sells itself when it creates this experience:

2. The site makes the problem specific.
3. The diagnostic confirms the problem.
4. The result gives a clear path.
5. Free exercises give a small win.

The site should not scream “buy my course.”

It should make the user think:

> This finally shows me what is wrong with my English.

> You know basic English, but you lack service phrases, polite structures, and problem-solving language. Start Hospitality English A2/B1.

## 30. Codex / AI Build Rules

AI can code this, but not if asked to “build the platform” all at once.

Codex should receive small, scoped tasks.

Every Codex prompt should say:

```text
Read CODEX_CONTEXT.md and docs/sblocco-product-strategy.md first.
Do not inspect unrelated files unless necessary.
Do not hardcode content inside JSX.
Do not create duplicated exercise components.
Do not create separate components for each level unless they are thin wrappers around shared renderers.
Do not implement tags as closed enums.
Do not hardcode logic for specific tag names inside components.
Do not create a second A1 topic component.
All exercises must use shared item schemas.
All diagnostic scoring must use flexible diagnostic metadata.
Known tags may be enriched through registries.
Unknown tags must not break rendering, scoring, diagnostics, or recommendations.
Recommendations should use registry mappings when available and fall back gracefully when not.
```

Strict non-negotiables:

```text
No hardcoded A1-only logic.
No duplicated exercise renderers.
No content directly inside JSX.
No closed tag enums.
No brittle if/else trees based on specific tags.
No fixed page structure that blocks future paths.
No diagnostic that only returns a score.
No generic “review auxiliaries” feedback.
```

## 31. What We Must Not Forget

- Sblocco Inglese is a platform + courses + premium support system.
- It should not become a random collection of pages.
- Levels must be practical and output-based.
- A1 must enable first speaking, not just grammar recognition.
- Hospitality is a major commercial track.
- Diagnostics are the selling engine.
- Diagnostics must be detailed, evidence-based, and context-aware.
- Tags must be open strings grouped by stable dimensions.
- Registries enrich tags but must not restrict them.
- Unknown tags must not break the app.
- The site should route users by blocker and use-case.
- The platform should always tell the learner what to do next.
- Human support remains premium and valuable.
- Frontend + localStorage is enough for MVP, but backend will be needed for accounts, paid access, cross-device progress, dashboards, and serious membership.
- Build the engine first, then one A1 vertical slice, then the first diagnostic, then Hospitality.
